About Me

My name is Cole Stotland and I am an Industrial Engineer studying at the University of Toronto. I am remarkably passionate about technology, and love to learn. I've always been a bit of a nerd, as my whole life I have loved video games, super-heros, and technology in general. This is what led me to begin working on my own projects. I find it extremely rewarding building things on my own and seeing them come to fruition. When I begin a project I am passionate about, I become extremely determined, sometimes to a fault, as I become restless until it's complete. My strengths include computer science, math, physics, and chemistry. I can code in many different languages such as Java, Python, R, C#, Javascript, CSS, HTML, C, SQL, and MATLAB and am very capable using programs such as Excel, Unity, Photoshop, Word, and iMovie.

I am also an avid sports fan. I played in a high level (AA) of hockey my whole life, and at one point even won defensive player of the year on a top 5 team in Ontario. I also take interest in many other sports, such as basketball, soccer, football, frisbee, and tennis. Evidently, I love to play any sport that is on land; just don't put me in the water!

I consider myself to be quite friendly and outgoing, so please feel free to reach out if you're interested in speaking with me!

Gale-Shapley Algorithm

The Gale-Shapley Algorithm is a well know method that optimally matches suitors to receivers. I was able to develop the algorithm using Java to optimally match students and schools designed by the user. The algorithm can easily be extended, however, to be applied in many different ways. Take a closer look at the code here!

COVID-19 Contact Tracing

During my first year in university, I worked on a program using Python that could conceptually help limit the spread of COVID-19 through contact tracing. The program takes in a CSV file of patient information and is able to decipher who directly and indirectly infected the most people, as well as who were cautious enough to avoid spreading it. Take a closer look at the code here!

Efficacy Of The COVID-19 Vaccine

One of my personal favourite projects was using data analytics, statistical modelling, and advanced calculus to quantitatively prove the impact of vaccines. This was achieved using a model that is common in predicting the spread of an illness known as the SIR (Susceptive, Infective, Recovered) model. By comparing the province with the lowest vaccination rate in Canada (Alberta) to that with the greatest vaccination rate (Newfoundland and Labrador), I analyzed how the spread (beta) and infectious period (gamma) changed based on vaccination rate. My research strongly suggests that vaccines lower both the infectious period and spread of the disease (see image below). To see the paper in more detail click here!

Stochastic Financial Modelling

This project was particularly interesting as I got a chance to apply my mathematical abilities to economics. By treating direct costs as variable costs following a PERT distribution, rather than using a fixed-cost method, and by using Excel I was able to calculate the expected net present worth of a company in 15 years' time.
